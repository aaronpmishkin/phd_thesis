\textbf{Convex Reformulations}:
Several attempts have been made to extend convex reformulations to deep
neural networks using restricted architectures.
\citet{ergen2021revealing} show that deep linear networks with scalar outputs
satisfy strong duality.
%They also characterize the solutions to regularized training problems for deep
%ReLU networks as extreme points of a convex set.
At the same time, \citet{ergen2021beyond} show that three-layer ReLU
networks arranged in parallel (also called multi-branch)
architectures have a convex reformulation which can be solved in polynomial
time when the network width is fixed.
Follow-up work by \citet{wang2023parallel} extends both of these results;
they prove that deep linear networks with vector outputs have a non-zero
duality gap and derive convex reformulations for deep parallel networks
with modified regularization.
They also show that three-layer ReLU networks with rank-one inputs satisfy
strong duality.

An alternative approach has been pioneered by \citet{pilanci2024clarity}, who
use geometric algebra to show that training deep ReLU networks can be cast as a
lasso problem over a dictionary of wedge-product features generated by the
training data.
They give equivalent lasso problems for two- and three-layer networks and
characterize the structure of optimal weights for arbitrarily deep models.
\citet{wang2024randomized} combine these results with sketching to perform
transfer learning in large language models.
These approaches do not rely on enumerating activation
patterns and so are substantially different from our results.

\textbf{Optimality Conditions}:
A separate line of work tackles the problem of deriving optimality conditions
for deep neural networks.
While validating the global optimality of a candidate solution is NP-Hard
for general non-convex programming \citep{murty1987complete},
\citet{jadbadbaie2018checking} study linear neural networks and prove that if
the product of the parameter matrices at a critical point is full-rank, then 
that point is also a global optimum.
They also provide several weaker results for deep non-linear networks using a
function space perspective.
In comparison, our deep convex reformulations are stronger because they come
equipped with a mapping between convex and non-convex solutions which can
always be used to evaluate the optimality of a candidate solution.
